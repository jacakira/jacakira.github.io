Compact Operators and Spectral Intuition

February 26, 2025

#math.functional-analysis · #math.operator-theory · #math.hilbert-spaces · #math · #math.analysis

Functional analysis has a reputation for being abstract and “soft”.
But once you internalise compact operators on Hilbert spaces, a lot of the theory suddenly feels almost finite-dimensional again.

This post is a somewhat opinionated walkthrough of:

  • compact operators on Hilbert spaces,
  • the structure of their spectrum, and
  • how to think about them using finite-dimensional approximations and a bit of code.

Along the way we’ll sprinkle some numerical experiments and quotes, to make it feel less like lecture notes and more like a working notebook.

1. Banach vs Hilbert: setting the stage

We’ll work mostly on a complex separable Hilbert space $H$, although some statements hold in general Banach spaces.

Recall:

  • A bounded linear operator $T : H \to H$ satisfies $$ |T| ;=; \sup_{|x| = 1} |Tx| ;<; \infty. $$
  • The spectrum $\sigma(T)$ is $$ \sigma(T) = {\lambda \in \mathbb{C} : T - \lambda I \text{ is not invertible}}. $$

“The spectrum is the shadow that an operator casts on the complex plane.”
— loosely attributed to various functional analysts

In finite dimensions, $\sigma(T)$ is just the set of eigenvalues. In infinite dimensions, all hell tries to break loose — but compact operators behave almost as if we were still finite-dimensional.

2. Compact operators: definitions and first properties

2.1 Definition

A bounded linear operator $T : H \to H$ is called compact if it maps bounded sets to relatively compact sets, i.e.

For every bounded set $B \subset H$, the closure $\overline{T(B)}$ is compact in $H$.

Equivalently (and more concretely):

$T$ is compact iff for every bounded sequence $(x_n)$ in $H$, the sequence $(Tx_n)$ has a convergent subsequence.

This already feels like “finite dimensional behaviour”: in $\mathbb{C}^n$, bounded sets are relatively compact; compact operators mimic that property.

2.2 Basic examples

  1. Finite rank operators are compact.
    If $\dim(\operatorname{im} T) < \infty$, then $T$ is compact.

  2. Integral operators on $L^2$ with square-integrable kernel.
    Let

    $$ (Tf)(x) = \int_0^1 K(x,y) f(y),dy, \qquad f \in L^2([0,1]), $$

    where $K \in L^2([0,1]^2)$. Then $T$ is compact on $L^2$.

  3. Inclusion maps between Sobolev spaces.
    The inclusion $H^1_0(\Omega) \hookrightarrow L^2(\Omega)$ is compact if $\Omega$ is a nice bounded domain (Rellich–Kondrachov).

“Compactness is the poor person’s finite dimensionality.”
— every FA lecturer, at some point

3. Spectrum of a compact operator

The key theorem to remember is:

Theorem (Structure of the spectrum of a compact operator).
Let $T : H \to H$ be compact. Then:

  1. Every non-zero $\lambda \in \sigma(T)$ is an eigenvalue of finite multiplicity.
  2. The only possible accumulation point of $\sigma(T)$ is $0$.
  3. If $H$ is infinite-dimensional, then $0 \in \sigma(T)$.

Schematic picture:

  • $\sigma(T)$ is either ${0}$, or
  • $0$ plus a sequence $(\lambda_n)$ of eigenvalues with $\lambda_n \to 0$.

Suppose $\lambda \neq 0$ and $(T - \lambda I)$ is not invertible. We want to find $x \neq 0$ such that $Tx = \lambda x$.

  • Show first that $\operatorname{im}(T - \lambda I)$ is closed and has finite codimension. This uses compactness and the Fredholm alternative.
  • Then $(T - \lambda I)$ fails to be injective or surjective.
  • In either case, you can produce a nonzero solution of $(T - \lambda I)x = 0$.

This is essentially the infinite-dimensional echo of the finite-dimensional fact “if a matrix is not invertible, $0$ is an eigenvalue”.

4. Self-adjoint compact operators: almost diagonal matrices

On a Hilbert space, the jewel in the crown is the spectral theorem.

Theorem (Spectral theorem for compact self-adjoint operators).
Let $T : H \to H$ be compact and self-adjoint ($T = T^*$). Then there exists an orthonormal basis $(e_n)$ of $H$ and a sequence of real numbers $(\lambda_n)$ with $\lambda_n \to 0$ such that

$$ T x = \sum_{n} \lambda_n \langle x, e_n \rangle e_n, \qquad \forall x \in H. $$

Equivalently, $T$ is unitarily diagonalizable with real eigenvalues accumulating only at $0$.

So in the right basis, $T$ looks like a diagonal matrix with entries $\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \dots$ tending to $0$.

This is why compact self-adjoint operators feel like “infinite symmetric matrices with eigenvalues decaying to $0$”.

5. A concrete example: an integral operator on $L^2[0,1]$

Consider the operator

$$ (Tf)(x) = \int_0^1 \min(x,y), f(y),dy, \qquad f \in L^2([0,1]). $$

One can show that $T$ is self-adjoint, positive, and compact. In fact, it corresponds to the Green’s operator for the 1D Laplacian with Dirichlet boundary conditions.

The eigenfunctions and eigenvalues are given by

$$ e_n(x) = \sqrt{2}, \sin(n \pi x), \qquad \lambda_n = \frac{1}{(n\pi)^2}, \qquad n \in \mathbb{N}. $$

So

$$ T f = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \lambda_n, \langle f, e_n \rangle e_n, $$

with $\lambda_n \to 0$ quadratically.

Let’s approximate $T$ on a grid and look at its eigenvalues. We’ll discretise $[0,1]$ into $N$ points and approximate the integral by a Riemann sum.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def build_T_matrix(N: int) -> np.ndarray:
    """
    Discretise the operator
        (Tf)(x) = ∫_0^1 min(x, y) f(y) dy
    on a uniform grid of N points in [0, 1].
    """
    x = np.linspace(0.0, 1.0, N)
    dx = 1.0 / (N - 1)

    # K_ij = min(x_i, x_j)
    X_i, X_j = np.meshgrid(x, x, indexing="ij")
    K = np.minimum(X_i, X_j)

    # Approximate integral by Riemann sum: T ≈ K * dx
    return K * dx

N = 200
T = build_T_matrix(N)

# Compute eigenvalues (since T is symmetric, use eigh)
evals, evecs = np.linalg.eigh(T)

# Sort in descending order
evals = np.sort(evals)[::-1]

plt.figure()
plt.semilogy(evals, marker="o", linestyle="none")
plt.title("Eigenvalues of discretised T (semilogy)")
plt.xlabel("index")
plt.ylabel("eigenvalue (log scale)")
plt.show()
